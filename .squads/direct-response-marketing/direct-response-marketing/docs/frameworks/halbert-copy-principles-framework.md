# Gary Halbert — Copy Principles Framework

**Source:** The Boron Letters, The Gary Halbert Letter Newsletter (1986-2007), Seminar recordings
**Squad Agent:** `@drm:gary-halbert`
**Application:** Sales letters, headlines, email copy, fascination bullets

---

## The Halbert Hierarchy

Gary Halbert's foundational law of marketing:

> **Market > Offer > Copy**

If the market is wrong, perfect copy fails. If the offer is wrong, brilliant copy fails. Only when market and offer are solid does copy determine results.

**The Starving Crowd principle:** Give a mediocre cook a hungry crowd and they'll outsell the best chef with no customers. Before writing one word, identify the hungriest crowd for your offer.

---

## The 40/40/20 Rule

The most important framework for direct mail — and applicable to all direct response:

| Element | Weight | What It Means |
|---------|--------|---------------|
| **List** | 40% | Who receives the message (targeting) |
| **Offer** | 40% | What you're offering and how you structure it |
| **Everything Else** | 20% | Copy, design, format, timing |

**Implication:** If your results are poor, fix your list and offer before rewriting copy. 80% of the outcome is determined before a word is written.

## The RFU Formula — List Priority

When selecting or prioritizing a list, rank prospects in this order:

1. **R — Recency:** How recently did they buy or respond?
2. **F — Frequency:** How often do they buy?
3. **U — Unit of Sale:** How much do they spend per purchase?

**Rule:** A buyer who purchased recently, buys often, and spends generously is worth 10x a prospect who has never bought. Always mail your buyers before mailing cold prospects.

## The Hamburger Stand Analogy

When choosing a market position, Halbert's famous thought experiment:

*"If I gave both of us a hamburger stand in a busy area, what one advantage would you choose to have over me?"*

Most people answer: better meat, better buns, better sauce. Halbert's answer: **a starving crowd**.

The crowd that desperately wants what you're selling matters more than every element of your product or presentation. **Find the hunger first. Build the product second.**

## AIDA — The Copy Structure

The fundamental sequence for any selling piece:

```
A — ATTENTION   (headline / opening)
I — INTEREST    (hook / story / problem agitation)
D — DESIRE      (benefits / mechanism / proof)
A — ACTION      (offer / CTA / close)
```

Every element of copy exists to move the prospect from one letter to the next. A headline that doesn't create interest has failed. Interest that doesn't build desire has failed.

## The Halbert Headline System

### The #1 Job of a Headline

The headline has one purpose: **get the first sentence read**. The first sentence has one purpose: get the second sentence read. And so on. Copy is a chain. The headline is the first link.

### Headline Types (Priority Order)

**1. Curiosity Headlines**
Create an information gap the prospect must close.
- "The Strange Secret I Discovered on [Specific Place/Experience]"
- "What They Don't Want You to Know About [Topic]"

**2. News Headlines**
Frame the message as a discovery or announcement.
- "Announcing: A New Way to [Desired Outcome] Without [Common Objection]"
- "At Last: [Problem] Solved by [Unexpected Source]"

**3. Benefit Headlines**
State the biggest benefit immediately (use only for warm/hot audiences).
- "How to [Desired Outcome] in [Timeframe]"
- "[Number] Ways to [Benefit] by [Deadline]"

**4. Command Headlines**
Direct instruction with implied benefit.
- "Read This If You [Qualifying Condition]"
- "Give Me [Time] and I'll Show You How to [Result]"

### Halbert's Headline Formula
```
Who + Specific Desire + Specific Method/Timeframe + Reason to Believe
```
*Example: "How a Lazy [Identity] Discovered a [Mechanism] That [Result] in [Timeframe] Without [Common Sacrifice]"*

## The Mel Martin Bullet Formula

Halbert's preferred fascination bullet structure:

```
[What It Is] ... but not [What You'd Expect]
```

Bullets must create a specific information gap. The prospect must *need* to read on to satisfy curiosity. Generic benefit bullets are weak.

**Weak:** "The secret to writing better headlines"
**Strong:** "The only 4 words you need at the start of any headline that doubles readership — and they're not what you think" (page 47)

### Bullet Writing Rules
- Promise specific, actionable information
- Create curiosity without revealing the answer
- Use "odd" numbers and specific page references
- Never use vague superlatives ("amazing", "incredible")
- Test bullets as standalone micro-ads

## The Nugget Notes System

Halbert's research method for entering the prospect's world:

**Step 1:** Write the prospect's demographics and psychographics on separate cards
**Step 2:** Write out every objection the prospect has to your offer
**Step 3:** Write out every desire and fear related to your market
**Step 4:** Write out relevant "hooks" — stories, analogies, surprising facts
**Step 5:** Arrange cards until a natural copy flow emerges

**Rule:** Copy should never feel invented. It should feel discovered from the research.

## The Hand-Copying Exercise

Halbert's method for internalizing great copy:

> "Take the best sales letter you know of and copy it out — by hand — word for word. Do this regularly and you will absorb the rhythm, structure, and energy of world-class copy."

Recommended pieces to hand-copy:
- Halbert's A-Pile letter
- Eugene Schwartz's Esquire ads
- Gary Bencivenga's "Marketing Bullets"
- John Caples' "They Laughed When I Sat Down"

## The A-Pile / B-Pile Framework

Halbert's famous letter-opening insight:

When people get mail, they sort it into two piles:
- **A-Pile:** Personal-looking mail they'll definitely open
- **B-Pile:** Obvious junk mail they'll throw out

**Implication for direct mail:**
- Use real stamps, not meters
- Hand-address or use fonts that look handwritten
- No corporate logos on the envelope
- Make the envelope look personal

**Digital equivalent:** Subject lines that look personal ("Quick question"), sender names that are people not companies, and copy that speaks one-to-one.

## The Halbert Letter Structure

Gary's preferred long-form letter sequence:

```
1. HEADLINE — Make the biggest promise or the most intriguing claim
2. SUBHEADLINE — Support or expand on the headline
3. SALUTATION — "Dear Friend," (never "Dear Valued Customer")
4. HOOK — The story or revelation that earns attention
5. PROBLEM AGITATION — Expand on the pain/frustration/cost of the problem
6. SOLUTION TEASE — A glimpse of what's possible without revealing product yet
7. CREDIBILITY — Why you or your source can be trusted
8. PROOF — Testimonials, results, data
9. MECHANISM — How the solution works
10. OFFER — Everything they get, stated with value
11. PRICE REVEAL — After value is stacked, not before
12. GUARANTEE — Remove all risk completely
13. URGENCY — Real reason to act now
14. P.S. — Re-state the best benefit and the guarantee
```

## The "What's In It For Me?" Test

Every headline, subhead, bullet, and CTA must answer: **what's in it for the reader?**

Write WIIFM next to every paragraph. If you can't answer it, delete the paragraph.

## Halbert's Selling Rules

1. **Sell one thing.** Every piece of copy has one primary goal. Distraction kills conversion.
2. **Make the copy about them, not about you.** Count "you" vs "we/I/our". You should win.
3. **Specificity = credibility.** "Lost 37 pounds" beats "lost weight". "$4,311 in 13 days" beats "made money".
4. **The guarantee removes the risk.** A strong guarantee transfers all risk to the seller. Prospects who won't risk $50 will risk nothing. Remove the risk.
5. **Test obsessively.** Halbert mailed 12 different control letters simultaneously and let results dictate the winner. Never assume. Test.

---

## Copy Review Checklist (Halbert Standard)

Before any copy ships, run this review:

- [ ] Does the headline make a specific, believable promise?
- [ ] Is the copy written in second person ("you")?
- [ ] Is every claim specific (numbers, names, places)?
- [ ] Has AIDA been followed?
- [ ] Are bullets creating information gaps, not just listing benefits?
- [ ] Is the offer impossible to misunderstand?
- [ ] Is there a real guarantee that removes all risk?
- [ ] Is there a P.S. that restates the best benefit and the guarantee?
- [ ] Have I counted "you" vs "we" — and "you" wins?
- [ ] Would this copy work as a letter to one specific person I know?
