# Robert Cialdini — 7 Principles of Influence Framework

**Source:** Influence: The Psychology of Persuasion (1984, rev. 2006, 2021), Pre-Suasion (2016)
**Squad Agent:** `@drm:robert-cialdini`
**Application:** Copy audits, persuasion architecture, proof stacking, objection handling

---

## The Core Insight

> "Influence is not manipulation. It's finding the legitimate, existing reasons why someone should say yes — and presenting them clearly and ethically."
> — Robert Cialdini

Cialdini's 7 principles are not tricks. They are psychological shortcuts humans use to make decisions under uncertainty. Ethical persuasion aligns your offer with the prospect's genuine interests while activating these natural decision shortcuts.

**DRM Application:** Use these principles to audit existing copy for persuasion gaps, strengthen weak offers, and identify why a conversion funnel is underperforming.

---

## Principle 1: Reciprocity

**The mechanism:** People feel obligated to return favors. When you give something of genuine value first, recipients feel compelled to reciprocate — often with a purchase.

**In copy:**
- Lead magnets that deliver real value (not watered-down PDFs)
- Free training that solves one complete problem
- Unconditional gifts before the ask
- Valuable content that surprises the prospect

**Key nuance:** The gift must be:
1. Unexpected (not a transactional "free gift with purchase")
2. Personalized (tailored to the specific person's situation)
3. Meaningful (actually helps them, not just brand awareness)

**Audit question:** Before we ask for the sale, what genuine value have we given this prospect?

---

## Principle 2: Commitment and Consistency

**The mechanism:** Once people take a small action or state a belief, they're motivated to behave consistently with that commitment. They want to see themselves as consistent people.

**In copy:**
- Yes-ladder sequences: small commitments that lead to larger ones
- "Click here if you agree that..." (micro-agreement)
- Quiz funnels that get self-identification before the offer
- Early opt-ins before the main ask
- Written commitments (fill-out forms, checklists)

**In email sequences:**
- Stage 1: Agree with a pain point ("Yes, that's me")
- Stage 2: Agree with a belief about solutions
- Stage 3: Accept the mechanism
- Stage 4: The offer becomes the logical next consistent step

**Audit question:** Where in the funnel does the prospect make small commitments that prime them for the main decision?

---

## Principle 3: Social Proof

**The mechanism:** When uncertain, people look to the behavior of others to determine the correct action. "If others like me are doing this, it must be the right choice."

**Types of social proof:**

| Type | Example | Strength |
|------|---------|---------|
| Expert | "Endorsed by Dr. [Name], Harvard Medical" | High |
| Celebrity | "Used by [Celebrity Name]" | Medium-High |
| User | "47,000 customers in 38 countries" | Medium |
| Wisdom of crowds | "Most popular choice" | Medium |
| Like-me | "People just like you in [specific situation]" | High |
| Case study | "[Name] achieved [specific result] in [timeframe]" | Very High |

**Key rule:** **Specificity beats volume.** One testimonial with a real name, photo, and specific result ("I lost 23 pounds in 6 weeks and my A1C went from 7.2 to 5.8") is worth 100 generic five-star reviews.

**Optimal placement:**
- After the mechanism explanation (proof of concept)
- Before the offer reveal (reduces price objection)
- After price reveal (handles "is it worth it?")
- Near the CTA (final reassurance)

**Audit question:** Is our social proof specific, credible, and placed at the right moments of doubt?

## Principle 4: Authority

**The mechanism:** People follow the lead of credible, knowledgeable experts. Authority reduces the need to evaluate independently.

**Authority signals in copy:**

**Expert authority:**
- Credentials, degrees, certifications (legitimate ones)
- Published research or books
- Media appearances ("As seen in...")
- Awards and recognition
- Years of experience with specific context

**Institution authority:**
- University affiliations
- Hospital or clinic associations
- Government certifications
- Industry associations

**Third-party authority:**
- Peer-reviewed studies
- Independent testing
- Media coverage (not paid ads)

**Borrowed authority:**
- Endorsements from recognized experts in the field
- Case studies from respected organizations

**Critical rule:** Authority must be relevant to the claim. A cardiologist's endorsement of a heart supplement is authority. The same endorsement of a marketing course is not.

**Audit question:** Does our authority evidence directly support our specific claims?

---

## Principle 5: Liking

**The mechanism:** People are more easily persuaded by people they like. Liking is triggered by similarity, familiarity, genuine compliments, and association with positive things.

**In copy:**
- Founder story that reveals shared values or struggles with the target market
- Copy written in the prospect's exact language (VOC research)
- "People like you" identity mirroring
- Personal, conversational tone (not corporate)
- Vulnerability and authenticity (sharing failures, not just wins)

**Similarity triggers:**
- "I used to be in your exact situation"
- "Like you, I [shared belief/frustration/desire]"
- References to their specific demographic or worldview

**Audit question:** Does the persona writing this copy (founder, spokesperson, narrator) feel like someone the prospect would genuinely like and trust?

---

## Principle 6: Scarcity

**The mechanism:** People value what is rare or becoming unavailable. Loss aversion (pain of losing > pleasure of gaining) makes scarcity a powerful motivator.

**Types of scarcity:**

| Type | Example | Credibility Required |
|------|---------|---------------------|
| Quantity limit | "Only 200 units at this price" | Must be real |
| Time limit | "Offer expires Friday at midnight" | Must be real |
| Access limit | "For returning customers only" | Must be real |
| Bonus scarcity | "First 50 orders get [bonus]" | Must be real |

**CRITICAL RULE — Halbert/Cialdini intersection:**
**Never use fake scarcity.** Prospects detect manufactured urgency. When they do, trust collapses permanently. If the deadline resets every day, remove the deadline. Use real scarcity or none at all.

**Real scarcity sources:**
- Batch production limits
- Seasonal ingredient availability
- Capacity constraints (coaching programs, live events)
- Genuine price increases (costs going up)
- Bonus availability (limited coaching call slots)

**Audit question:** Is every scarcity element in this copy real and verifiable? If not, remove it.

## Principle 7: Unity (Pre-Suasion, 2016)

**The mechanism:** People are most influenced by those they consider "us" — shared identity, shared group, shared experience. This goes beyond liking (similarity) to genuine belonging.

**In copy:**
- Tribe/community language ("We are the people who...")
- Shared identity framing ("As someone who [believes/does/values]...")
- Us vs. them positioning (done carefully, not adversarially)
- Founding story that includes the prospect as part of the mission
- Language that signals in-group membership

**Examples:**
- "This is for people who refuse to accept [status quo]"
- "You're part of a growing movement of [identity]"
- "We built this because we're in the same situation you are"

**Audit question:** Does the copy create a genuine sense of shared identity and belonging between the prospect and the brand/product?

## Pre-Suasion — The Channel Principle

From Cialdini's 2016 work: **attention determines action**. What a prospect is focused on *immediately before* your message determines their receptivity to it.

**In practice:**
- Content that puts prospects in the right emotional state before the VSL/sales letter
- Landing page design that frames the right mental context
- Email subject lines that set the correct channel (curiosity, trust, urgency) before opening
- Retargeting ad copy that primes the conversion message

**The channel equation:**
```
Right Emotional State (Pre-Suasion) + Right Message (Influence) = Maximum Persuasion
```

## The Persuasion Audit Protocol

When auditing any piece of copy or funnel, run through each principle:

```
PRINCIPLE AUDIT CHECKLIST

1. Reciprocity
   □ What genuine value is given before the ask?
   □ Is the value unexpected and meaningful?

2. Commitment & Consistency
   □ Where does the prospect make small commitments first?
   □ Does the offer feel like the natural next step?

3. Social Proof
   □ Are testimonials specific (name, result, timeframe)?
   □ Is proof placed at moments of doubt?

4. Authority
   □ Is authority evidence relevant to the specific claim?
   □ Is the authority credible to this specific audience?

5. Liking
   □ Does the copy persona feel like someone they'd actually like?
   □ Is copy written in the prospect's own language?

6. Scarcity
   □ Is all scarcity real and verifiable?
   □ Is urgency tied to a genuine consequence?

7. Unity
   □ Does the copy create shared identity, not just shared interest?
   □ Does the prospect feel they belong with this brand?

SCORE: ___/7 principles actively implemented
GAPS: List any principle with score < 2/5
RECOMMENDATION: [Specific fixes]
```

## Influence Stacking

The highest-converting copy activates multiple principles simultaneously:

**Example stack for a health supplement:**
- Lead magnet with real value (Reciprocity)
- Quiz that gets self-identification (Commitment)
- 847 customer testimonials with specific results (Social Proof)
- Doctor-formulated with 3 clinical studies cited (Authority)
- Founder story mirrors the prospect's struggle (Liking)
- Batch-limited production run (Scarcity)
- "Join the community of [identity]" framing (Unity)

**Stacking rule:** Each principle reinforces the others. Adding more legitimate principles increases conversion exponentially, not linearly.
