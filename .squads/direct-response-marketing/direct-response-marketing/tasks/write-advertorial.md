# Task: Write Advertorial

**Agent:** eugene-schwartz
**Tier:** 1
**Type:** Creation
**Elicit:** true

## Purpose
Create native advertorials — content that reads like editorial journalism but converts
like direct response copy. Eugene Schwartz's principle: the advertorial meets the reader
where they already are (consuming content) and guides them naturally toward the offer.

## Prerequisites
- Awareness stage diagnosed (diagnose-awareness-stage.md) — Stage 2-3 markets respond best to advertorials
- Avatar defined (create-customer-avatar.md)
- Publication or platform identified (determines editorial voice to match)
- Mechanism defined (develop-unique-mechanism.md)

## Inputs Required
- Publication/platform (Facebook feed, news site, health blog, etc.)
- Awareness stage of target audience
- Product being promoted
- Primary mechanism or insight to share
- Proof elements available (studies, testimonials, data)

## The Advertorial Formula

An advertorial is not a softer sales letter. It is journalism that sells.
The reader must never feel deceived — the value must be real, the insight genuine.
The commercial intent is present but never dominant.

**Ratio rule:** 70% editorial value / 30% commercial intent (maximum)
**Tone rule:** Match the publication — if it's a health blog, write like a health journalist

## Execution Steps

### Step 1: Select the Advertorial Angle
The angle determines the entire structure. Choose based on awareness stage:

**Angle A — Scientific Discovery (Stage 2-3 markets):**
Format: "Researchers at [institution] discovered [counterintuitive finding]..."
Best for: Health, supplements, financial products
Hook: The discovery that explains WHY common approaches fail

**Angle B — Investigative Journalism (Stage 3 markets):**
Format: "I spent 6 months investigating why [common solution] doesn't work..."
Best for: Business, marketing, relationships
Hook: The investigation that reveals the hidden truth

**Angle C — Personal Story (All stages):**
Format: "I was diagnosed with [condition] in 2019. What happened next..."
Best for: Any niche with strong emotional component
Hook: Relatable struggle that leads to discovery

**Angle D — Expert Interview (Stage 3-4 markets):**
Format: "[Expert name], a [credential], has a controversial view on [topic]..."
Best for: Authority-driven niches (finance, health, legal)
Hook: The expert who says what everyone else won't

**Angle E — List/Tips (Stage 1-2 markets):**
Format: "7 Signs Your [Common Solution] Is Actually Making [Problem] Worse"
Best for: Education-first markets
Hook: Pattern interrupt that creates new awareness

### Step 2: Write the Headline and Deck

**Headline principles:**
- Must look like an editorial headline, not an ad
- Specific and surprising — creates cognitive dissonance or curiosity gap
- Contains a number OR a counterintuitive claim OR a specific promise
- Does NOT mention the brand or product

**Strong advertorial headlines:**
- "The Hidden Reason 91% of People Who Exercise Still Can't Lose Weight (It's Not What You Think)"
- "A 47-Year-Old Financial Advisor Discovered a Tax Strategy the IRS Doesn't Advertise"
- "Why Scientists Are Calling This 'The Most Misunderstood Health Condition of the Decade'"

**The Deck (subtitle):**
2-3 sentence expansion of the headline. Sets up the story without revealing the answer.
Example: "New research challenges everything we thought we knew about [topic]. The implications for [avatar] could be significant — but the medical establishment isn't talking about it."

### Step 3: Write the Editorial Lead
The first 3 paragraphs must be pure journalism — no selling, no brand mention.

**Lead structure:**
1. **The Hook Paragraph:** State the counterintuitive finding or open the story
2. **The Stakes Paragraph:** Why does this matter? What are the consequences?
3. **The Setup Paragraph:** What is the conventional wisdom that's being challenged?

Example lead (health/supplements):
"For the past decade, millions of Americans have been told that exercise and diet are the
keys to managing their blood sugar. But a growing body of research suggests that for many
people — particularly those over 45 — this advice may be dangerously incomplete.

The consequences of ignoring the emerging science can be serious. Uncorrected blood sugar
dysregulation is now linked to cognitive decline, cardiovascular disease, and significantly
shortened lifespan.

Yet most doctors continue to focus exclusively on diet and exercise, missing what some
researchers are calling 'the missing piece' in metabolic health."

### Step 4: Build the Editorial Body (70% of total length)

**Structure:**
1. **The Problem (expanded):** Go deeper into what the reader already knows
2. **Why Common Solutions Fail:** The mechanism explanation (educate, don't sell)
3. **The Discovery/Research:** Present the core insight with evidence
4. **Expert Perspective:** Quote or paraphrase a credible source
5. **Real-World Application:** How does this apply to the reader's daily life?
6. **The Gap:** What is still missing? What tool/approach addresses this?

**Key rules:**
- Every claim is supported (study, expert, data, anecdote)
- No superlatives ("best," "most amazing," "revolutionary") — use neutral journalist language
- Acknowledge complexity — "this isn't a magic solution, but the research suggests..."
- Use real names, real institutions, real data wherever possible

### Step 5: Write the Commercial Transition
The moment where editorial becomes commercial. Must feel natural, not abrupt.

**Transition formulas:**
- "Given this research, some [practitioners/people/companies] are taking a different approach..."
- "This is precisely the problem that [Product Name] was designed to address..."
- "It was this insight that led [Founder Name] to develop [Product Name]..."
- "For [Avatar Name], the turning point came when she discovered [Product Name]..."

**What NOT to do:**
- Never abrupt: "CLICK HERE TO BUY" immediately after editorial content
- Never contradict: don't trash everything you just educated them about
- Never oversell: "this cures everything" — stay within the editorial tone

### Step 6: Write the Commercial Section (30% max)
Brief, factual description of the product positioned as the editorial solution:

- What it is (in simple terms)
- How it works (connecting back to the mechanism discussed in the editorial)
- Who it's for (avatar specific)
- Key proof points (testimonial or result)
- Simple CTA with low friction ("Learn more" or "See how it works" — not "BUY NOW")

### Step 7: Compliance and Disclosure
Every advertorial requires appropriate disclosure:

```
ADVERTISING DISCLOSURE: This article is sponsored by [Brand Name].
The information provided is for educational purposes.
[Required health/legal disclaimers as applicable]
```

Disclosure must be:
- Visible (not hidden in fine print)
- At the beginning OR clearly marked as "sponsored content"
- Compliant with FTC guidelines and platform policies

## Quality Standards
- Passes "editorial test": could this be published as-is without the commercial section?
- Ratio maintained: 70% editorial, 30% commercial (maximum)
- Headline is specific and editorial in tone
- Every claim in editorial section is supported
- Commercial section flows naturally from editorial content
- Disclosure visible and compliant

## Veto Conditions
- VETO if headline reads like an advertisement ("Buy This Product Today!")
- VETO if commercial section exceeds 40% of total length
- VETO if editorial content contains unsupported health/financial claims
- VETO if disclosure is missing or hidden
- VETO if brand is mentioned in the first half of the piece
- VETO if mechanism is vague ("it just works better")

## Completion Criteria
- [ ] Angle selected with rationale (matched to awareness stage)
- [ ] Headline and deck written (editorial tone, high curiosity)
- [ ] Editorial lead (3 paragraphs, pure journalism)
- [ ] Full editorial body (mechanism explained, evidence cited)
- [ ] Commercial transition (natural, not abrupt)
- [ ] Commercial section (product positioned as editorial solution)
- [ ] Disclosure written and positioned
- [ ] Content compliance check (platform/regulatory)
